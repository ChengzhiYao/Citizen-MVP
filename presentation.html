Why I built this

Citizen tells you what's happening — but doesn't help you avoid it

What feels off today

You pay to control your own alerts. Choosing which alerts you get, and where, is a paid (Premium) feature.

It floods you with everything, everywhere. A stream of citywide incidents — widely criticized for making people more anxious, not safer.

You don't know what happens after you post. When I tried Go Live, it never said how long to record, or where the video ends up.

The “people who look out for you” part just sits there. Safety Network, friends & location sharing exist — but they're passive settings, not something that helps in the moment.

What Guardian does instead

The real problem isn't missing features — staying safe just takes too many separate steps. Nobody sets up five tools while walking home scared.

Guardian does it all in one tap: it picks your safest route, shares your live location with people you trust, and warns you about incidents on your path — before they reach you.

From an app that makes you anxious → one that actually helps you get home safe.
The AI · where it earns its place

AI turns messy human reports into a calm, trustworthy signal

AI for understanding & prediction. Rules for safety-critical actions.

Rerouting is geometry; geofencing is deterministic — you don't want an LLM hallucinating a reroute or a 911 call. AI doesn't replace the rules — it compresses chaos into a signal the rules can safely act on.

    Product judgment & reality

    What I deliberately didn't build — and what production demands

    Chose not to build

    ML area risk-prediction — rejected. Crime data encodes policing bias; predicting “this area is risky” is algorithmic redlining. AI stays on specific, real-time incidents — no area/demographic profiling.

    Registered addresses — gated. Public data, but opt-in, off by default, no names, de-alarmed, “not proof of danger.”

    Driving mode — cut. Can't out-navigate Google; focused on the pedestrian wedge it ignores.

    MVP → production, honestly

    Prototype stack: a web app with a server-side AI layer, realtime shared state, and a lightweight rules engine for geofencing / relevance / safety thresholds.

    Harder in production: native app support for background location + push; trusted incident pipelines; moderation & false-positive handling; scoped auth, consent & retention; and a model strategy — hosted, in-house, or on-device — based on latency, cost, privacy & safety.

    Real, working, deployed — with AI placed where it earns its value and kept out of where it's dangerous.

    The core design conversation — building Guardian

    Paraphrased from the real back-and-forth. I came in with the direction — and the stack (Vercel · Supabase · DeepSeek were my calls); these exchanges were me pressure-testing feasibility, difficulty & build order. My calls in blue.

    Me
    Citizen's safety tools are all there but scattered — you configure each one separately. I want to unify them into one proactive companion: one tap that runs the safe route, live sharing and monitoring together. Call it Guardian.
    Claude
    Clear direction — scoped it as one flow so the setup disappears.
    Me
    Scope is walking-only. Driving's a losing fight against google/waze on ETAs, and the real unmet need is the on-foot moment. No reason to widen it — agree?
    Claude
    Agree — drivers are lower-risk and well-served; the pedestrian gap is the defensible wedge. Locked scope to walking.
    Me
    good — and walking-only keeps the threat model tight: i'm not competing as a nav app, i'm covering the stretch where someone actually feels exposed on foot.
    Me
    On routing — we can't guarantee a safe path, maps recalculates anyway. So we pass a waypoint and label it “safer route”, never “guaranteed”. I want that honesty built in from the start.
    Claude
    Built the waypoint handoff with that framing + an upfront disclaimer.
    Me
    And to be clear why this isn't just reskinning maps: it's a different objective function. Maps optimizes ETA on traffic; we route on personal-safety incidents it never ingests. That's the whole reason Guardian exists.
    Claude
    Right — same map, different objective. Baked that into the routing logic.
    Me
    No clickable mockup — it has to actually run: real geolocation, a compressed-time walk sim, the AI reacting live. I'm hosting on Vercel (static + serverless) — confirm we can do the model call server-side so my key never ships to the client.
    Claude
    Confirmed & built — real geolocation + compressed sim-time + a rules risk-engine; the model call runs in a Vercel serverless function, key server-side.
    ↳ guardian.html
    Me
    the route's cutting straight through blocks — you're densifying a straight line, not following the street graph. switch to real routing (OSRM), and add a fallback for when the API's down so the demo never dead-ends.
    Claude
    Switched to real street routing (OSRM) with a fallback.

    The AI, ethics & privacy decisions

    Me
    My rule: anything safety-critical stays deterministic — routing and geofencing can't ride on an LLM that might hallucinate a reroute. The AI's only job is what rules can't do: turn messy human reports + scanner chatter into one calm, classified signal. I'll wire DeepSeek for that.
    Claude
    That's the split — rules own the safety-critical path, DeepSeek reads the reports. Wired it into a serverless function, key server-side.
    ↳ DeepSeek server-side
    Me
    the “predict risky areas” idea worries me — train on historical incident data and you just relearn policing bias and redline whole neighborhoods. i don't want to ship that. keep the AI on live, specific incidents, not area scores.
    Claude
    You're right — rejected ML area-prediction as algorithmic redlining; kept AI to specific, real-time incidents only.
    Me
    citizen surfaces registered offenders too… if we use that it has to be opt-in, no names, and make clear a listing isn't proof of danger
    Claude
    Gated it exactly that way — opt-in, off by default, de-alarmed, no names, with the caveat.
    Me
    SOS has to be manual — never auto-send my location to 911, that's a privacy line. and add a check-in: every few min i confirm “i'm ok”; only if i miss it, warn my contacts
    Claude
    Manual SOS confirm with clear consent; added the timed check-in.
    Me
    Add live watchers: I share a link, generated when I start, with a waiting screen showing who joined, then I walk — they see my live location + ETA.
    Claude
    Built the invite link + join/wait screen — real names & count, “X joined” alerts.
    Me
    For the two-device realtime I'll use Supabase — Vercel's stateless, so it can't hold shared state between two phones, right? The watcher needs my live route (updating on reroute), a history, and mid-walk joins.
    Claude
    Correct — Vercel's stateless, so Supabase Realtime holds the shared state. Built the live route + activity/route history + mid-walk joins.

    Appendix · architecture

    How the pieces fit

    Vercel — host + compute
    • Serves guardian.html / watch.html
    • Serverless function calls the LLM — key stays server-side
    • Stateless: can't hold data or push realtime
    Supabase — realtime state
    • Postgres + Realtime channels
    • Live location, incident injection, watchers, event feed
    • The two-device job a function can't do
    OSRM + DeepSeek
    • OSRM: free real street routing
    • DeepSeek: LLM incident understanding
    • Leaflet + CartoDB dark tiles for the map

    In one line: Vercel = hosting + the LLM function; Supabase = realtime shared state between two phones; OSRM = real routes; DeepSeek = the AI understanding.
